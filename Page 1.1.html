Fiction Techniques


  • Show, Don't Tell:
    Instead of telling the reader what's happening, show them through action and dialogue.
  • Imagery:
    Use vivid descriptions to create mental images for the reader.
  • Symbolism:
    Use objects, colors, or other elements to represent abstract ideas or concepts.
  • Foreshadowing:
    Hint at events that will happen later in the story.
  • Flashback:
    Use a scene or narrative that takes the reader back in time.
  • Dialogue:
    Use conversation between characters to reveal their personalities, relationships, and motivations.
  • Pacing:
    Control the speed at which the story unfolds to create tension, suspense, or relaxation.
  • Point of view:
    Choose the perspective from which the story is told, such as first person, third person limited, or omniscient.
  • Character development:
    Create well-rounded characters with their own motivations, desires, and flaws.
  • Plot twist:
    Use an unexpected turn of events to surprise the reader and add complexity to the story.
  • Red Herring:
    Use a false clue or misleading information to distract the reader from the true plot.
  • Unreliable Narrator:
    Use a narrator who is not trustworthy or who withholds information from the reader.
  • Stream-of-Consciousness:
    Write in a flowing, unstructured style to mimic the natural flow of thoughts and feelings.
  • Alliteration:
    Use repetition of initial consonant sounds to create a musical or rhythmic effect.
  • Allusion:
    Reference a person, place, event, or work of art outside the text itself to add depth and meaning.
  • Hyperbole:
    Use exaggeration for emphasis or effect.
  • Metaphor:
    Compare two unlike things without using "like" or "as."
  • Simile:
    Compare two unlike things using "like" or "as."
  • Personification:
    Attribute human qualities or characteristics to non-human entities, such as objects or animals.
  • Streamlining:
    Use concise language and eliminate unnecessary words to create a more efficient narrative.
